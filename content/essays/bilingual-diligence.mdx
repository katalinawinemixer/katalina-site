There is a CRO call I sat in on, between a US sponsor's clinical operations lead and the Brazilian site management team running a multi-center oncology trial. The sponsor's lead was on a translator service. The Brazilian PM was on a video link. The conversation went, in summary, well: deliverables on track, enrollment trending toward target, no flagged risks. Forty-five minutes. Both sides hung up satisfied.

The call had been, in the original Portuguese, materially different. The Brazilian PM had used a phrase &mdash; *estamos correndo atrás* &mdash; that the translator rendered as "we're working hard." The Portuguese phrase translates literally to "we are running after" and idiomatically means: *we are behind, we are scrambling, we will probably get there but it is not on track*. That signal &mdash; *we are scrambling* &mdash; is the signal a sponsor's clinical operations lead exists to receive. The translator's "we're working hard" buried it.

This is the cheapest version of the bilingual diligence asymmetry. The expensive versions are worse.

## The translator problem is structural

When a US biotech investor underwrites a Latin American CRO's operational footing, or evaluates a LatAm-incorporated biotech's clinical-stage trial, the language of analysis is English. The CRO's reports are in English. The pitch deck is in English. The interviews with the management team are in English, conducted by the management team's English-speaking executives. The translator, when one is used, is hired by the company being evaluated.

This is fine for the headline story. It is not fine for the parts of the story where pattern recognition lives &mdash; tone, idiom, what was *not* said, what was said in passing. A management team that uses *vamos ver* (let's see) repeatedly when asked about delivery dates is signaling something different than a management team that uses *vamos cumprir* (we will fulfill). A CRO that describes its inspection-readiness using the word *organizado* (organized) instead of *auditado* (audited) is making a different claim. A regulatory affairs lead who says *temos uma boa relação com a ANVISA* (we have a good relationship with ANVISA) is saying something specific about how the relationship is maintained &mdash; and what that maintenance looks like is worth a follow-up question that an English-speaking diligence call will never ask.

None of this is hidden. It is just legible only to people who hear the language at native cadence.

## What you actually catch in Spanish

I have been on diligence calls &mdash; informally, as a friend &mdash; for two LatAm biotech evaluations. In each case, the asymmetry was not "I caught a hidden problem." It was finer than that.

In one, a Mexican CRO's regulatory lead described the timeline for the company's next IND-equivalent submission to COFEPRIS using the phrase *si Dios quiere* (God willing). The phrase is colloquial in Mexico in a way that doesn't necessarily indicate uncertainty &mdash; it can be a verbal tic. But in the regulatory affairs context, used by a senior regulatory lead, in answer to a timeline question, *si Dios quiere* signaled that the lead did not control the variables that would determine the timeline. That was the question worth asking next. The English-translated transcript of that call rendered the phrase as "we hope so."

In the other, a São Paulo biotech's CMO described the company's ongoing Phase&nbsp;Ib trial enrollment using the phrase *está caminhando bem* (it's walking along well). The translator rendered this as "going well." *Caminhando* is a softer word than *indo bem* (going well) &mdash; it specifically connotes pace rather than progress, and in a Phase&nbsp;Ib enrollment context, it is the word you use when enrollment is happening but slower than internal targets. The investor who heard "going well" thought the trial was on track. The trial, on closer questioning, was four weeks behind.

Bilingual diligence is not about catching a lie. It is about hearing the cadence of the truth.
</PullQuote>

## Why the asymmetry persists

There are three reasons most US biotech firms underwrite LatAm operations through a translator and don't notice that they are doing it.

First, the firms that have LatAm exposure are mostly geography-generalist firms with one or two associates assigned to LatAm dealflow. Those associates are usually English-Spanish bilingual but not Portuguese, or they have basic Spanish but not the regulatory and clinical-operations vocabulary that makes the diligence productive. The vocabulary takes years to build. Most firms don't invest in it because the dealflow doesn't yet justify the headcount.

Second, the LatAm biotechs that are most legible to US firms are the ones that have already English-localized their team and their materials. The LatAm biotechs that haven't &mdash; the ones operating primarily in Portuguese and Spanish, where the asymmetry would be most valuable to bridge &mdash; are also the ones least visible to US dealflow. The selection is biased.

Third, the value of bilingual diligence compounds with on-the-ground time. A diligence call where you understand the original language is more useful than one in translation. A site visit where you can have the side conversations is more useful than one chaperoned by management. A relationship with a regulatory lead built over two years of bilingual conversations produces the kind of information that doesn't show up on diligence calls at all. Most US firms can't afford that compounding curve, and so they outsource it to local CROs that have their own commercial reasons for keeping the picture simple.

## The window

The asymmetry is real and persistent, but it is not permanent. Two things will erode it.

The growing class of bilingual operator-investors, especially in São Paulo, Mexico City, and Miami, is going to make the asymmetry less valuable to a single firm and more valuable as a market. The LatAm-focused funds (Kaszek, Monashees, Cometa, ALLVP) are building biotech competency on top of fintech-and-consumer dealflow. As they do, the firms with bilingual operating partners will become the natural co-investors on cross-border deals.

The maturation of LatAm regulatory infrastructure &mdash; ANVISA, COFEPRIS, INVIMA &mdash; will produce a class of regulatory affairs documentation that is genuinely useful in English. As that happens, more of the legible diligence will move into a language US firms can read. The asymmetry will narrow.

The window in which a Spanish-and-Portuguese-fluent operator can underwrite cross-border bio with materially better information than a generalist firm is the next five to seven years. That is plenty of time to build a track record. It is also a finite amount of time, and the operators who position now &mdash; in dealflow, in personal network, in published thinking &mdash; will be the ones the firms call when the dealflow is too valuable to ignore.

I am writing some of this down because the asymmetry is going to close, and I would like to be in the room before it does.
