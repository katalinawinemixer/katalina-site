A São Paulo oncology site I am familiar with enrolls patients into a Phase&nbsp;II solid-tumor trial in six weeks that takes a comparable US site twenty-six. The molecule is the same. The protocol is the same. The principal investigator at each site is roughly comparable in stature. The difference is that the Brazilian site has a referral pipeline from public-health hospitals that channels four hundred eligible patients per month past its screening clinic, of whom a meaningful fraction are treatment-naïve, and the US site is competing for the same patients with twelve other studies at three other academic centers within a thirty-mile radius. This is the boring answer to why people are running clinical trials in Latin&nbsp;America in 2026, and it is the answer that most US biotech investors still treat as exotic.

I want to argue that Latin&nbsp;America is going to be the most interesting place in the world to run a Phase&nbsp;I trial for the next ten years. The argument has four parts.

## The treatment-naïve advantage is structural, not temporary

In the US, the median oncology patient enrolling in a Phase&nbsp;II trial has had two prior lines of therapy. In some heavily-trialed indications &mdash; relapsed multiple myeloma, refractory NSCLC &mdash; it is four. Each prior line creates noise: it changes the patient's mutation profile, it produces toxicities that obscure the new agent's safety signal, and it raises the bar for what counts as efficacy.

The structural difference in Latin&nbsp;America is not that patients are sicker; it is that the public-health systems direct patients through a different access ladder. Many patients enrolling at a Brazilian or Mexican academic site are first-line eligible for indications where US sites can almost never find first-line patients. Sponsors who want a clean efficacy signal in a heavily-trialed indication should be enrolling those patients. Most sponsors aren't, because their site-selection process inherits from a US-centric playbook that treats LatAm as the place you go for cost.

Cost is real. It is a third-order reason at best.

## The genetic diversity advantage is starting to matter

Until recently, biotech could ignore genetic diversity in clinical trials with relative impunity. The FDA cared less than it cares now, the major payers cared less than they care now, and the regulatory framework treated demographic representation as a soft requirement. That has shifted. The 2022 FDA Diversity Action Plan guidance, the 2023 update, and the increasing payer scrutiny of label populations have made it expensive &mdash; not just embarrassing &mdash; to bring a drug to label with a trial population that doesn't reflect the disease population.

Latin American populations &mdash; admixed European, African, and Indigenous ancestries in proportions that vary substantially across countries &mdash; produce trial cohorts that resolve diversity requirements without sponsors having to architect them. A Brazilian site enrolls Afro-Brazilian and European-ancestry patients in a single referral stream. A Mexican site enrolls patients with significant Indigenous-ancestry contribution. For trials in indications where pharmacogenomic differences across ancestry matter &mdash; oncology, cardiometabolic, several CNS indications &mdash; this is not a soft asset. It is a label asset.

## "World-class CRO" is not a coastal-elite category

The single most common mistake I hear US-based biotech investors make about LatAm clinical trials is the assumption that the operational infrastructure is scrappy. It isn't. The major Brazilian academic centers run trial portfolios of comparable scale and complexity to mid-tier US academic medical centers. The CROs operating across LatAm &mdash; both global firms with regional teams (IQVIA, ICON, Parexel) and regional specialists (Hospital Albert Einstein in São Paulo, the network around Hospital Universitario in Mexico) &mdash; have GCP-trained staff, validated systems, and inspection records that compare favorably to US peer sites.

What is genuinely different is the institutional layering. A US Phase&nbsp;I trial at an academic site usually involves the site, a contract CRO doing monitoring, a central IRB, the sponsor's regulatory team, and the FDA. A Brazilian Phase&nbsp;I trial involves the site, a contract CRO, the site's own ethics committee (CEP), the national ethics body (CONEP), and ANVISA. The number of institutional handoffs is higher. The discipline required to manage them is also higher.

The sites that win in this market are the ones that have built the muscle to do that handoff work fast. The CROs that lose are the ones that try to overlay a US-process model on a LatAm institutional structure.

## What's actually changing in 2026

Three things are new enough to matter for investors building a LatAm thesis right now.

The first is that ANVISA's review timelines for first-in-human studies have compressed materially since 2023. ANVISA is not yet faster than FDA on average, but the gap is closing, and for biologics in oncology indications it is in some cases narrower than US-based sponsors expect.

The second is that COFEPRIS &mdash; Mexico's federal health regulator &mdash; has been undergoing a structural reform agenda that includes clinical-trial review process modernization. There are real implementation challenges; this is not finished. But the direction is clear, and Mexico-based biotech and CRO infrastructure is rebuilding around the new framework.

The third is that the Latin American venture ecosystem has a growing class of biotech-adjacent operators &mdash; ex-CRO leaders, ex-pharma regulatory leads, MD-investors &mdash; who are increasingly active as scouts and angel investors in cross-border deals. The dealflow is becoming legible to LatAm-focused funds (Kaszek, Monashees, Cometa, ALLVP) in a way it wasn't five years ago. US bio funds are still mostly absent.

## Three categories of company that should be doing more LatAm trials

**Cell and gene therapy companies running Phase&nbsp;I/II trials in oncology.** The treatment-naïve advantage compounds with the diversity advantage. A São Paulo or Mexico City site that enrolls fast, with a representative cohort, can de-risk the next financing round more cheaply than any US site of equivalent stature.

**Companies developing therapies in indications with high public-health prevalence in LatAm.** Chagas, leishmaniasis, dengue &mdash; obviously. Less obviously: NAFLD/MASH, where Latin American population prevalence is high; certain hematologic indications where ancestry distributions matter; type-2 diabetes complications where population age and treatment-naïveté both favor LatAm enrollment.

**LatAm-first biotechs.** A small but real cohort of biotechs are now choosing to incorporate in the US, run their core team binational, and run their pivotal trials primarily through LatAm sites. This is a thesis. It is most legible at funds with explicit LatAm health programs (FEMSA Ventures, Hi Ventures' bio bets, several family offices in São Paulo and Mexico City).

The argument I want to leave you with is this. Latin&nbsp;America is not the cheap place to run trials. It is, increasingly, the *good* place to run trials &mdash; for specific indications, with specific cohort requirements, under a regulatory framework that is improving rather than stagnant. Sponsors and investors who understand this in 2026 will look prescient by 2030.

Most of the work to see it requires the same thing US biotech investors already do for any other geography: read the regulatory updates, talk to operators on the ground, and visit the sites. The barrier is mostly that the conversations happen, in many cases, in Portuguese and Spanish, and most US firms have outsourced their fluency to a CRO that has reasons of its own to keep the picture simple.
