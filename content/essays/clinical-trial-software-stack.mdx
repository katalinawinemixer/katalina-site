Clinical-trial software is usually sold to the person who wants visibility and used by the person who needs to get through the day.

That gap explains a lot.

The buyer wants dashboards, compliance, traceability, integrations, workflows, and proof that the trial is under control. The coordinator wants to know where to upload the delegation log, why the monitor cannot see the document, whether the query actually saved, and how many clicks it takes to answer one question without breaking another field.

I have used enough of these systems to believe the demo matters less than the implementation.

## Veeva and the document truth problem

Veeva can be powerful because it gives the sponsor a controlled document environment. It can also become a beautiful warehouse of things nobody can find quickly.

The question is not whether Veeva exists. The question is whether the study team has naming discipline, version discipline, training discipline, and a clear owner for the document lifecycle.

An eTMF is not inspection-ready because it is digital. It is inspection-ready because every important document has a home, a date, a version, and a reason it belongs there.

## Florence and the site reality

Florence eReg is closer to the site experience. That matters.

A site is not thinking in sponsor categories all day. A site is thinking about what needs to be signed, filed, trained, reconciled, or shown to a monitor before the next visit. Software that respects that workflow feels different from software that only mirrors a sponsor checklist.

The best eReg tools reduce the number of private spreadsheets a coordinator has to keep alive. The worst ones create one more system that has to be reconciled manually.

## OnCore is infrastructure, not magic

OnCore is one of those systems that becomes invisible when it is working and unbearable when it is not.

It can hold protocol calendars, subject tracking, financials, and operational status. But like every CTMS, it is only as good as the discipline around it. If visit tracking is late, calendars are wrong, or statuses are treated as decoration, OnCore becomes a place where the official story slowly separates from reality.

That separation is where operational risk lives.

## EDC tools are where clean data is negotiated

Medidata and PreClarus sit closer to the data story. They are also where the phrase "clean data" becomes less comforting.

A field can be complete and still require judgment. A query can be answered and still reveal a process issue. A missing source value can turn a simple data point into a week of emails. EDC systems are not just databases. They are the place where site behavior, monitor expectations, sponsor pressure, and protocol design collide.

Good teams watch query aging and query type. Great teams ask what the queries are teaching them about the trial.

## The software diligence question

If I were evaluating a clinical-trial software company, I would not start with the product deck.

I would ask:

- who uses it every day
- which workflow it replaces
- what spreadsheet still exists beside it
- what happens when sponsor, CRO, and site permissions collide
- how long a new coordinator needs before they stop making mistakes
- whether the system makes inspection readiness easier or just more legible

The answer should be specific. If it sounds like "single source of truth" without explaining whose truth, I would keep digging.

## Implementation is the product

Clinical-trial software does not win because it has the most complete feature list. It wins when the implementation fits the trial's actual operating system.

That means permissions, training, naming conventions, escalation paths, migration decisions, sponsor expectations, and the ugly edge cases nobody puts in the sales demo.

The product is not only the software. It is the workflow the software makes possible.

## The investor takeaway

Most eClinical diligence should get closer to the user.

Ask the sponsor what visibility it gained. Then ask the coordinator what work disappeared. Ask the monitor what got easier to verify. Ask the data manager what got cleaner. Ask the site what spreadsheet still refuses to die.

The truth is usually in that last answer.
