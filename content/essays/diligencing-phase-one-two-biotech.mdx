If I were diligencing a Phase I/II biotech company, I would start with the same assumption I use at the site level: the molecule can be interesting and the trial can still be badly built.

Early clinical diligence should not stop at mechanism, market, and management. Those matter. But the next layer is whether the company can run the trial it is using to prove the story.

## Start with the patient

I would ask the company to describe the first ten patients they expect to enroll.

Where do those patients show up? Which clinics see them? Are they treatment-naive, relapsed, refractory, biomarker-selected, heavily pretreated, geographically concentrated, or already competing for other trials? What does the screening pathway look like before consent?

If the answer becomes abstract quickly, I would worry. A real enrollment plan has a map. It has referral sources, screen-fail assumptions, competing studies, and a reason each site belongs in the trial.

## Read the protocol for friction

The protocol is where I would look for hidden execution risk.

Eligibility criteria can be too narrow. Visit schedules can be too heavy. Sample handling can be fragile. Imaging windows can be unrealistic. Required biopsies can quietly destroy conversion from screening to enrollment. A sponsor may think it has designed a rigorous trial when it has actually designed one that only a few sites can execute well.

I would ask which protocol requirements the sites pushed back on. Sites complain for a living, but useful complaints tell you where the plan is brittle.

## Ask about amendment cycle time

I would ask for the average time from amendment finalization to site activation under the new version.

That one number tells you a lot. It captures regulatory affairs, IRB responsiveness, site training, consent updates, sponsor communication, and whether the company can keep the trial moving without losing document control.

Fast does not always mean good. But slow without a clear reason is expensive.

## Look at deviations before the inspection does

Every trial has deviations. I care about the pattern.

Are deviations concentrated at one site? Are they mostly visit-window issues, eligibility issues, drug-accountability issues, consent issues, or data-entry issues? Are they being closed on time? Are corrective actions real or just words in a field?

An early clinical company that understands its deviation pattern understands its own trial. A company that treats deviations as low-level site noise is asking to be surprised later.

## Ask who owns the systems

I would ask who lives in the CTMS, who lives in the EDC, who owns the eTMF, and who reconciles them.

The answer matters because clinical systems do not magically agree with each other. A patient can exist in one system before another. A document can be final in one place and missing in another. A site can appear activated in the sponsor dashboard while still waiting on a local training record.

When systems disagree, someone has to notice. That someone is part of the risk profile.

## Treat geography as strategy, not decoration

If the company is running outside the US, I would ask why each country is there.

Latin America can be a serious clinical advantage: treatment-naive populations, strong oncology investigators, referral networks, and improving regulatory pathways. It can also be used lazily, as a cheaper enrollment story without enough local operating knowledge.

The question is not "are they using LatAm?" The question is whether they understand the sites, regulators, languages, and patient pathways well enough to make that choice an advantage.

## What would make me more confident

I would feel better if the company could explain:

- which sites are doing the real enrollment work
- what changed after the first amendment
- how deviations are reviewed and closed
- where screen failures are coming from
- which operational assumption has already been wrong
- how the team knows the eTMF is inspection-ready before anyone asks

I would feel worse if every operational question got sent to the CRO.

The CRO can run tasks. It cannot care more about the company than the company cares about itself.

## The investor takeaway

Phase I/II diligence should ask whether the company knows its own trial at the level where things break.

Not just the endpoint. Not just the biology. Not just the KOL quotes. The site list. The patients. The amendment path. The deviations. The systems. The country choices. The parts of the work that decide whether the company gets evidence or just spends money trying.

That is where I would start.
