The inspector arrived at 8:47 on a Tuesday in April. Two thousand pages were already pulled and waiting on a long table in the conference room — protocols, amendments, monitoring reports, training records, signed 1572s, IRB approvals, deviations and their reconciliations. He had asked for them three days earlier in an email that said, in summary, *bring everything*.

The coordinator who tells you the binders ahead of an inspection should be alphabetized is correct but missing the point. The inspector does not read the binders. He pulls one page, asks a question that connects three unrelated documents, and watches your face. The whole game of an inspection — the part most sites do not understand until their second one — is whether the documents you maintained over the previous eighteen months can answer questions nobody thought to ask while they were being made.

We had three days of this. He left with no critical findings. I want to talk about why.

## The version-control problem

The single most predictive thing about whether a site survives an inspection is whether it can answer, instantly, this question: *which version of the protocol was in effect on this specific patient visit?* It sounds trivial. It is not. A protocol that goes through five amendments in fourteen months produces a chain of documents in which each amendment supersedes a specific date range, requires its own IRB approval, requires its own consent form revision, requires its own training acknowledgement from every coordinator and investigator on the study, and propagates across the eTMF, the IRB system, the CTMS, and the sponsor's own systems &mdash; each of which has its own clock, its own definition of "in effect," and its own opinion about what counts as a date.

Sites lose inspections in this gap. The inspector pulls a visit note from a Tuesday seventeen months ago, asks which version of the consent form was used, and the site has to demonstrate &mdash; in real time, in front of a federal officer &mdash; that the version stamped on the signed ICF matches the version that was IRB-approved on that date, that the patient signed the right amendment, and that the coordinator who took the consent had been trained on it. Every link in that chain has to hold.

The discipline that produces this isn't bureaucratic patience. It's a particular kind of paranoia. You assume, for every document you touch, that someone will pull it eighteen months later and ask you to prove it.

## What inspectors actually look for

Three things, in this order:

**One, can you find anything in under sixty seconds.** The inspector asks for a deviation log; you produce it. He asks for the corrective action that closed deviation #47; you produce it. He asks for the SAE narrative for subject 0312; you produce it. The speed isn't a courtesy. Slow retrieval is itself the finding &mdash; it tells him your eTMF is theatre, populated retroactively, not maintained as the actual operational record.

**Two, do your dates line up.** Every regulated document has at least two dates: when it was generated and when it became effective. An ICF amendment that was IRB-approved on March 4 cannot be the version used on a March 2 consent. A protocol amendment that takes effect on the date of the IRB approval letter cannot be cited in a deviation logged the day before. Sites that drift on dates are sites that have been faking it. Inspectors find this within minutes.

**Three, can your story survive being checked from three angles.** The inspector will ask the coordinator one version of a question, the investigator another, and look at the documents for a third. The three should match without needing to. If the investigator says he was trained on amendment 4 in February and the training log puts that at March, you have a problem you cannot talk your way out of.

## The deviation reconciliation black hole

Every Phase I–III trial has deviations. A patient takes a study drug at the wrong time, a lab draw is missed, a randomization stratum is filled out incorrectly. The deviations themselves are not the inspection risk. The reconciliation is.

Deviation reconciliation means: for every deviation in the log, has it been categorized (minor / major / important), has the root cause been documented, has the corrective action been implemented, has the closure been verified, and has the sponsor been notified per the protocol's reporting requirements. Sites lose inspections because deviations stay open in the system &mdash; logged but not closed &mdash; for months. The closure work is the unglamorous part. It involves emails to the investigator who recorded the deviation, follow-ups with the CRA, sometimes a re-training of a study coordinator. It is easy to defer. It is impossible to defer at scale and survive.

I run a quarterly deviation reconciliation on twenty-one studies. The reconciliation is not a check; it is an act of forced closure. Anything that has been open more than thirty days gets a name attached and a deadline. By the time the inspector arrives, there is nothing in the queue older than the protocol allows.

## The training records trap

Every person who touches a study has to be trained on it. That training has to be documented, dated, signed, and tied to a specific version of the protocol. When a protocol amendment changes anything &mdash; eligibility criteria, dose escalation rules, lab schedule &mdash; everyone on the study has to be re-trained on the change. The re-training has to happen before the amendment goes into effect. The records have to live in the eTMF and they have to match the personnel on the 1572.

This is where I see most sites fail at audit. A new sub-investigator joins the study in month nine; her name appears on a visit note in month ten; her training acknowledgement for the protocol in effect at month ten cannot be found. The inspector doesn't have to find a clinical issue. The missing training record is enough.

## What this means if you're underwriting

If you are an investor evaluating a Phase&nbsp;I biotech, ask three questions about the sponsor's regulatory operations &mdash; not the science.

How long does it take them, on average, from protocol amendment finalization to all sites having IRB approval and trained personnel? A well-run sponsor does this in fewer than thirty days. A poorly-run one takes ninety, and every day of that delay is a day patients are enrolling under outdated criteria.

What is their site selection process? Sponsors who pick sites for "geographic diversity" without checking the site's recent inspection history are picking sites that will surface their regulatory weaknesses to the FDA. Ask for the inspection history of the lead enrolling sites. It's public. Most associates never check.

What does their deviation reconciliation cycle look like? If the answer is "we close them out as they come in," the answer is wrong; high-quality sponsors run a quarterly forced closure on every site. If the answer is "we trust the sites," the sponsor has not been through an FDA inspection.

The inspector who walked into our conference room on a Tuesday in April left after three days. He pulled one hundred and forty pages over those three days. Every single one of them was where I said it would be.

That, I have come to believe, is what an FDA inspection actually rewards.
