The scientists running a biotech think their Phase&nbsp;I trial fails because of the molecule. The molecule fails too &mdash; about half the time, depending on the indication. But of the trials I have watched run aground, almost none failed because the molecule was wrong. They failed because something happened in the operational layer of the trial that nobody planned for, and the people responsible for that layer are not the people the investor diligenced.

This is a pattern book. Eight ways Phase&nbsp;I trials really fail, what each one looks like from inside the site, and the question I would ask any biotech I was thinking about underwriting.

## 1. Eligibility creep

The protocol's inclusion and exclusion criteria are written by the medical monitor in conversation with the sponsor's clinical lead, six to twelve months before the trial activates. By the time enrollment starts, the people writing those criteria have moved on to the next trial. The criteria collide with the patient population that actually walks into the clinic, and the site starts requesting protocol exceptions: this patient has a creatinine of 1.5 instead of 1.4, can we waive it; this patient had a different prior regimen than the protocol specifies, can we adapt.

Each individual exception is reasonable. The cumulative effect is that by enrollment closure, the trial population looks meaningfully different from the population the protocol was designed for, the data is hard to interpret, and the FDA sends an information request that delays the read-out by six months.

*Question to ask*: What is the rate of protocol exceptions on this trial? If the sponsor doesn't track it, that itself is the answer.

## 2. Site activation lag compounds

A Phase&nbsp;I trial typically activates eight to fifteen sites. Activation involves IRB approval, regulatory submissions, contract negotiation, training, and budget execution. The protocol is signed; the sponsor expects all sites to be enrolling within ninety days. In practice, sites activate on a long-tail distribution &mdash; the fastest at thirty days, the slowest at two hundred and ten.

The trial enrollment rate looks fine on the average. It is catastrophic on the variance. The fast sites enroll their full cohort in the first six months and stop. The slow sites are still ramping when enrollment closure should be approaching. The sponsor either closes early (smaller cohort, weaker signal) or extends the trial (longer timeline, more burn).

*Question to ask*: What was the median and p90 site activation time on the sponsor's last trial? A well-run sponsor will know. A poorly-run one will give you the median and ignore the p90.

## 3. The dose-escalation cohort that takes nineteen months

In oncology, the dose-escalation phase &mdash; usually a 3+3 or BOIN design &mdash; is the slow part of a Phase&nbsp;I trial. Each cohort is small, and you cannot enroll the next cohort until the safety review committee clears the previous one. The committee meets when patients have completed their dose-limiting toxicity window, which means the cadence is set by patient enrollment and follow-up time.

Trials get stuck here for reasons that have nothing to do with safety. A site enrolls a patient and then can't enroll the next one for ten weeks because the patient flow happens to slow. The committee can't convene because two members are on PTO. The next-cohort drug supply is delayed at customs. None of these is the molecule. All of them produce the same outcome: a Phase&nbsp;I trial that should take fourteen months takes thirty.

*Question to ask*: What is the median time from first patient dosed to dose-escalation complete in the sponsor's prior trials? Anything over eighteen months is a yellow flag for operational discipline.

## 4. The CRO mismatch

A small biotech with twelve people running its Phase&nbsp;I trial through a global CRO with thirty thousand employees gets a CRO project team that is exactly as senior as the biotech's purchasing power justifies. The biotech assumes that the CRO will run the trial the way it would run its own. The CRO assumes that the biotech knows what to ask for. The trial gets a project manager who has run two trials before, a lead CRA who is also covering three other studies, and a regulatory associate who is six months out of school.

The trial is not under-resourced; it is under-scoped. The CRO will follow the SOW exactly. Things that aren't in the SOW &mdash; the secondary endpoints that became important after the protocol was signed, the post-amendment training that everyone thought the sponsor was handling &mdash; fall through.

*Question to ask*: What is the named project manager's prior trial experience? If the answer is "we trust the CRO," the answer is wrong.

## 5. The translator problem in safety reporting

In multinational trials, every adverse event has to be reported in English to the sponsor, in the local language to the local regulator, and in some cases also to a sponsor's regional regulatory body. The translation chain is staffed by humans, on deadline, with judgment calls about what counts as related, what counts as serious, what counts as expected.

A serious adverse event reported in Portuguese as related to study drug becomes, by the time it arrives at the sponsor's safety database in English, ambiguously related. A SUSAR that should be reported to FDA in seven days instead arrives in fourteen. None of the people in the chain were negligent; the chain itself produced the lag.

*Question to ask*: Does the sponsor have a SUSAR/SAE workflow that can demonstrate end-to-end timing under fifteen calendar days for non-US sites? Most can't.

## 6. The lab kit problem

Every Phase&nbsp;I trial requires kits &mdash; tubes, vials, shipping containers, requisitions &mdash; sent from a central lab to each site, used for each patient visit, returned to the central lab for analysis. The kit logistics have to align with patient visits, with shipping windows, with customs in international sites, and with stability windows for the analytes being measured.

The kit problem looks small until you scale it. A trial enrolling forty patients with twelve visits each is four hundred and eighty visits, each requiring a unique kit, returning to a central lab on a schedule the sponsor doesn't fully control. When kits arrive late or in the wrong configuration, samples get processed wrong, the data is unusable, and patients have to be re-drawn.

*Question to ask*: Who is the named central-lab project manager and what is the kit-error rate on the sponsor's last trial? "Low" is not a number.

## 7. Investigator distraction

The principal investigator at a Phase&nbsp;I site is, in most cases, a senior physician with a full clinical practice, two to four other active studies, an academic appointment, and grant deadlines. The biotech believes the PI is engaged with the trial. The PI is engaged with the trial when there is a problem to solve. The rest of the time, the PI is delegating to a sub-investigator and a coordinator.

This is fine when the trial is well-run. It is a disaster when something goes wrong, because the PI's situational awareness is twenty-eight days out of date.

*Question to ask*: What is the cadence of sponsor-PI direct calls? Once a month is too rare for a Phase&nbsp;I trial.

## 8. The unblinded medical monitor in a small CRO

Phase&nbsp;I trials need a medical monitor &mdash; a senior MD reviewing safety data, available to the sites for clinical questions, decision-making on dose escalations and SAE reports. In a small CRO or a small biotech, the medical monitor is one person, often part-time, often retired, with judgment that becomes the sole bottleneck for clinical decision-making.

When that person is on vacation, or has a family emergency, or quietly disengages because they have too many studies, the trial does not stop. It runs without senior medical judgment for the duration. The sponsor doesn't notice until a sub-investigator asks a question and gets an answer two weeks late.

*Question to ask*: Who is the named medical monitor, what is their workload, and what is the backup arrangement? "We have coverage" is not an answer.

---

The pattern under all eight of these is the same: the molecule is not the bottleneck. The trial is a complex operation with a long chain of human and institutional handoffs, and any one of them can stall. Investors who underwrite the molecule and not the operation are underwriting half the risk.

The biotech investors who develop a sense for this stop saying "we believe in the science" and start saying "we believe in the operating discipline." It is the same bet, taken on the right axis.
