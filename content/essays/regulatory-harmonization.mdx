For most of the last twenty years, the regulatory pathways for clinical trials in Latin America were, from a US sponsor's perspective, three separate problems with three separate timelines, three separate document standards, and three separate conversations to manage. ANVISA in Brazil. COFEPRIS in Mexico. INVIMA in Colombia. ANMAT in Argentina. Each with its own clinical trial application, its own ethics committee structure, its own definition of a substantial amendment. A sponsor running a multi-country trial across LatAm was, in regulatory terms, running four trials.

That description is becoming wrong. Not all at once, not evenly, and not in a way that you would necessarily notice from a US biotech industry conference. But the regulatory pathways across the major Latin American clinical-trial markets are converging on each other, on ICH guidelines, and increasingly on the FDA's own framework. The convergence is far enough along that it is starting to be a sourcing thesis &mdash; not just for biotechs choosing where to run trials, but for investors deciding where the next decade of LatAm-first clinical-stage companies will come from.

This is a short field guide.

## What's actually happening at the regulators

ANVISA has been the most public mover. Over the last several years &mdash; through the COVID-era acceleration of biologics review, the post-2020 modernization push, and the more recent regulatory reform agenda &mdash; ANVISA has compressed clinical-trial application timelines, accepted ICH-formatted dossiers more readily, and, importantly, signed mutual recognition arrangements that allow it to leverage reviews from comparable regulators. The structural posture is: ANVISA wants to be a recognized stringent regulatory authority within ICH, and the trial-approval timeline is part of how it gets there.

COFEPRIS has been more uneven, but the direction is similar. The agency has gone through structural reform &mdash; including a pandemic-era backlog and an ongoing modernization push &mdash; and the implementation is ahead of the public reputation. For Phase&nbsp;I biologics in oncology specifically, the COFEPRIS pathway in 2026 is meaningfully faster than it was in 2018. The variance is high; the median is improving.

INVIMA, ANMAT, and the smaller regional regulators are following at roughly the speed of their domestic pharmaceutical industries' demand for first-in-human studies. The dynamic is the same: convergence on ICH, increasing acceptance of FDA- and EMA-format dossiers, modest compression of timelines.

The cumulative effect is that a sponsor running a Phase&nbsp;I/II trial across Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia in 2026 is operating in a regulatory environment where the *protocol* and *regulatory dossier* are largely portable across countries, the *ethics review* is still local, and the *timeline variance* is meaningfully narrower than it was five years ago.

## The biotech business models that benefit

If the convergence is real, three classes of company are positioned to benefit before US-centric biotech firms notice.

**LatAm-incorporated, US-clinical-stage biotechs.** Companies headquartered in São Paulo, Mexico City, Bogotá, or Buenos Aires that run their pivotal trials primarily through LatAm sites, with an FDA submission as the eventual regulatory destination. The convergence makes this model cheaper and faster than it used to be. The sponsors retain LatAm cost structure on operations, run trials across populations that are increasingly attractive to US payers, and ladder into FDA approval through pathways that are increasingly compatible with FDA expectations.

**US-incorporated biotechs running LatAm-anchored Phase&nbsp;I/II trials.** A growing pattern: US biotech with US team, US headquarters, US investor base, but with the operational center of its early-phase trials in São Paulo or Mexico City. The economics work because the convergence is reducing the cost-of-trial-management premium that used to apply to LatAm-anchored trials. The science works because the population advantages I have written about elsewhere are real.

**Cross-border CROs and clinical-trial software companies.** If you believe the convergence thesis, you should believe that the operational layer between sponsors and LatAm sites is going to grow faster than the underlying market. The regional CROs (the largest São Paulo and CDMX-based ones), the global CROs' regional teams, and a class of clinical-trial software companies that are starting to localize for ICH-Latam workflows &mdash; these are the picks-and-shovels bets.

## What investors should be tracking

The regulatory convergence isn't a single moment. It's a slope. The investors who price the slope before it's legible to the broader market get the asymmetric upside. Five things worth tracking, monthly or quarterly.

**ANVISA's clinical-trial approval timelines.** ANVISA publishes statistics on clinical-trial application review times. The trend over the last three years has been clearly downward. Watch the median, the p90, and the variance separately &mdash; all three matter.

**COFEPRIS's reform implementation.** The reform agenda has been announced; the implementation is ongoing. Watch the throughput numbers more than the announcements.

**ICH membership and observer status.** ANVISA and COFEPRIS are at different stages of formal ICH engagement. Movement here &mdash; observer to member, deeper engagement on specific guidelines &mdash; signals that the regulator is committing to a long-horizon framework. This is a leading indicator.

**Mutual recognition arrangements.** The bilateral and multilateral arrangements between LatAm regulators, and between LatAm regulators and FDA / EMA, are the structural plumbing of the convergence. Each new arrangement compresses the cost of running trials across borders.

**The composition of cross-border IND-IND-equivalent filings.** The filings themselves are not always public, but the ratio of trials being filed simultaneously to FDA and to ANVISA / COFEPRIS is a useful proxy. When a sponsor files in parallel rather than sequentially, it is taking a bet on the convergence.

## The sourcing thesis

The investor takeaway is narrow but specific. The convergence makes LatAm-anchored or LatAm-bridge biotech companies more investable today than they were five years ago, because the *regulatory friction tax* on running their trials has dropped and is dropping. A LatAm biotech in 2018 had to pay an operational complexity premium that materially worsened its capital efficiency. The same biotech in 2026 doesn't.

That changes the math on which LatAm biotechs deserve to be sourced and underwritten. It also changes the math on which US biotechs should be running LatAm-anchored trials. And it changes the math on the cross-border infrastructure layer &mdash; the CROs, the clinical-trial software companies, the regulatory-affairs services &mdash; that connects the two.

The sourcing thesis is: the firms that build credible LatAm bio operating capability between 2026 and 2030 will own a category that is going to look obvious in retrospect.

I am betting on the slope.
